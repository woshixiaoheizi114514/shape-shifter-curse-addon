# Soreth East Road

*A Full Nine-Chapter Story*

---

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 1

Chapter 1: The Iron-Rank Archer

————————

The southern plains of Caskonia have never lacked roads — nor the troubles that travel along them.

The city of Solaist sits at the meeting of three trade routes. Wheat fields and villages lie to the west, hill mines to the north, and to the east stretches an old royal road merchants call the "Solaist East Road." Once it was wide and even, paved with greyish-white stone, with a waystation set every half-day's march. But war, raids, monsters, and the political negligence no one cares to admit — these crawl across every proud human work like moss. The road still leads to Imeir Town, but it is no longer the easy stretch the bards once described as "a road you could walk to the eastern orchards with your eyes shut."

By day, wolf packs trail the scent of carts through the tall grass. At dusk, spiders rasp against the stone walls of abandoned mineshafts. At night, zombies stagger out of forgotten graveyards, and skeletons string their bows behind broken walls, patient enough to wait for a torch to burn out. The garrisons of Caskonia are not unwilling to clear them — only the city walls, the tax granaries, the border posts, and the manors of the nobility always come first on the list. So commoners and merchants pin their hopes elsewhere.

On the Mercenary Guild.

It belongs to Caskonia no more than to any other nation. Some say it began as a few retired soldiers patched together into a tavern guard. Others say great figures who refuse to show their faces stand behind it. Still others claim its vaults run deeper than the treasuries of more than one small kingdom. Whatever the rumour, almost every trade-rich city on the continent flies the same black banner with its silver sword.

The Guild takes coin and does the work — escorts, hunts, searches, forest sweeps, cave clearings. So long as the laws of the host country are not broken, and so long as the contract is signed and sealed, the job goes up on the board. The Guild has its own rank system, from lowest to highest: Stone, Charcoal, Iron, Gold, Diamond, Netherite, and Obsidian. Stone-ranks are mostly youngsters who have only just lifted a weapon. Charcoal-ranks can barely stand alone against the things that walk at night. Iron-ranks count as reliable. Gold-ranks are the ones who begin to gather a name and the right to choose their work. Above Diamond, those people seldom linger in taverns waiting for jobs — their names tend to appear on noble letters, royal warrants, or on the lists of certain altars no one openly speaks of.

The first time Ayla walked into the Solaist Mercenary Guild tavern, an iron badge hung at her belt.

The badge was nothing ornate — only a slightly worn iron plate stamped with a simplified bow and sword. But in the tavern, plenty of Charcoals and Stones still stole an extra glance at it. Iron meant she was not still alive on luck alone. She had cleared cave spiders from the North Hill mineshafts, ridden with caravans through the oak woods three times, and brought four trapped herbalists out of an abandoned watchtower in a thunderstorm. That last contract had left her right shoulder with an ugly scar — and made the Guild clerk write beside her name: "Cool judgment, precise archery. Suited for escort and scouting."

Ayla didn't much like that line. The word "cool" always sounded to her like something one wrote when there was nothing else to say.

She was an archer — lean, light-footed, always wearing a dark grey sash drawn over half a shoulder. Her bow was no master's work, only a composite of black oak, sinew, and a few slivers of wolf bone, but she kept it with obsessive care: bowstring always dry, fletching always trimmed, quiver sorted into ordinary arrows, fire arrows, armour-piercing arrows, and a few costly potion arrows. Some saw her silence and thought her easy to push around. Some saw her age and assumed she had reached Iron on luck. After one job with her, those people learned quickly to keep their mouths shut.

The mercenary tavern in Solaist is louder than an ordinary tavern, and more like a small market. The job board hangs to the left of the door, the registration counter to the right, and at the back stand the never-cold hearth and three rows of heavy wooden tables. Charcoal recruits cluster near the door, the better to lunge for easy contracts the moment they go up. Iron-ranks and Gold-ranks sit further in, away from the wind at the door and closer to the casks. Behind the counter is Bern, both barkeep and contract broker — bald, thick-bearded, an old scar above his left eye. He had been a mercenary too in his time, until a skeleton's arrow took him in the knee, and he retired to mind the Guild's tavern. He swore foully, but he remembered what every regular drank, and he remembered which men were never to be put on the same team.

The day Ayla walked through the door, she had just finished a forest-monster contract.

Mud caked her leather armour; pine needles still clung to the soles of her boots. The job had come from the lumber yard east of the city: monsters had been dragging horses off into the night. The Guild had originally sent three; in the end, only Ayla pushed deep into the woods. There she found two wolves, a knot of zombies drawn by the smell of carrion, and one enormous spider hiding in the canopy that had nearly turned the whole forest into a web-laced lair. She had spent half a quiver and a bottle of slowness potion before dawn dragged the spider's corpse to the lumber-yard gate — and pulled an apprentice out of the woodpile he'd been hiding in along the way.

The pay was modest. The record was beautiful.

Bern slid the coin pouch over and grinned. "Nice work, Iron. Keep this up and one day you might brush the door of Gold."

Ayla took the pouch and answered evenly: "Before that, I'd rather have a beer."

Someone laughed in the tavern. Ayla didn't turn. She found an empty stool by the bar, set down her bow, and started checking her fletching. She was no celebrant, and no boaster of contracts in front of a crowd. To her, returning, taking the pay, mending the gear, and sleeping — those four things were more solid than any praise.

But the Solaist tavern never lets anyone sit quietly for long.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 2

Chapter 2: The Gold-Rank Axeman

————————

Rayne came through in the evening.

Saying he "came through" puts it too gently. That oak door had survived more drunks' shoulders than anyone could count, taken impacts from raised shields, even been split once by a runaway chicken jockey — but in Rayne's hands it was light as a shingle. One push of the shoulder, and the door slammed against the wall, dragging in a cold wind and the smell of iron rust.

The noise in the tavern dipped for a moment.

Rayne was too easy to notice. Tall, with shoulders broad enough to plug a mine entrance, a heavy axe slung at his back. The haft was dark wood wrapped in worn leather; the edge had small nicks but had been polished mirror-bright. On his left arm rode an old steel shield, its face scorched in places, the family crest at its centre worn nearly to ghost by years and battle. The gold badge at his chest caught the firelight. Several recruits looked away at once.

Gold-rank.

In Solaist, Rayne was something of a name. He had escorted a column of miners alone through Skeleton Valley. He had cut down a young pillaging beast that had crashed into a sheepfold during a village raid. Rumour, as ever, made the man taller than the man himself: some said he could split an iron gate with one swing, some said he could carry a wounded man ten miles after three barrels of ale, some said his temper was foul enough that even zombies kept clear.

Ayla only believed the last one.

Because Rayne had barely stepped inside before his eyes settled on her table — on the spider venom gland and the coin pouch from her contract.

"Oh?" He walked to the bar, voice low but pitched to carry across half the room. "So this is the Iron-rank who soloed the lumber-yard cleanup? I thought I'd at least see something resembling a fighter."

Ayla didn't look up. She kept paring fletching with her knife, voice level. "Then you're looking in the wrong place. Fighters tend to sit somewhere louder, somewhere more in the way."

Someone in the tavern stifled a laugh.

Rayne raised an eyebrow. He hadn't really come to pick a fight — not at first. He had only just returned from a tedious, fiddly escort and was in poor humour, when he'd happened to overhear a few Charcoals making Ayla sound as if she could shoot the Ender Dragon out of the sky with a single arrow. Rayne hated that sort of inflation. He had seen too many young ones fed dizzy on praise — only to die in their first real night fight faster than a torch could burn out.

"Archer." He pulled out the chair beside her; the legs gave a pained groan. "Kill a few spiders in the woods on a lucky day, and now you think you can hold your own?"

Ayla finally raised her eyes. They were cold — not arrogant cold, but the clear kind left in someone who has spent a long time pressing fear and anger down into the bottom of themselves.

"Greataxeman," she said. "Cleave everything in two and now you think you understand combat?"

This time the laughter didn't stay in.

Rayne's face darkened a shade. He took a swallow of ale and said, low: "At least my way of fighting doesn't get my teammates used as targets up front."

Ayla's hand stopped.

She set the arrow back in the quiver, slowly, and turned around to face him fully.

"Say that again."

The tavern went quiet again.

Behind the counter Bern sighed. He knew that voice too well — when someone is only angry, the pitch climbs. When someone has been struck on a wound, the voice goes lower. Ayla's voice was the second kind. Rayne heard it too, but his pride wouldn't let him back off yet.

"I said," Rayne set the cup down, eyes on hers, "I don't let my teammates get used as targets up front."

The next instant Ayla's short knife stood quivering in the wood of the table, less than an inch from his fingers.

Rayne's axe came off his back in the same breath; the edge crushed an empty cup at the table's rim.

Charcoals scattered. Stones brightened as if someone had announced free entertainment. Brawls between Iron and Gold are uncommon in this tavern — especially when one of them is Rayne. Bets were already being offered on whether the archer could mark his face before being thrown out the door.

"Enough." Bern's voice cut from behind the counter.

No one listened. The egging-on went right on.

Bern slapped his rag down, took up the iron-banded rod from under the bar, and brought it down on the wood with a crack. "I said enough. The Guild tavern isn't a duelling pit. Want to fight, register at the city watch, post the bond, and find a legal witness. Otherwise both of you spend the night mucking stables — gold-rank included."

That worked better than any persuasion.

Rayne loosened his grip on the haft. Ayla pulled the knife free and slid it back into her boot. Neither apologised. They turned their faces away at the same moment, as if even one extra glance would dirty the air between them.

Bern looked at them, and something deeply unpromising came into his expression.

"As it happens," he said, "you two are full of energy. There's a contract that should burn some of it off."

Ayla frowned. "I just got back."

Rayne frowned. "I'm not taking anything tied to her."

Bern pulled a fresh sheet of vellum from below the board and slapped it on the counter. The wax seal on it had not yet fully set; it bore the double stamps of the Apothecaries' Guild and the Mercenary Guild.

"Unfortunate, then," Bern said. "The client asked for the two of you by name."

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 3

Chapter 3: Seventy Kilometers East

————————

The contract was simple — at least on paper.

Escort one apothecary and one cart of medicines from Solaist eastward to Imeir Town, seventy kilometres out. The apothecary was resupplying Imeir's clinic: potion bases, spider eyes, gold nuggets, redstone dust, blaze powder, nether wart, and several crates of glistering melon slices for brewing potions of regeneration. The goods were not cheap, but every item was logged and licensed under the Caskonia Apothecary Office. The route was fixed: the Solaist East Road, no straying through noble hunting grounds or military post roads. The escort was responsible for the apothecary, the cart, and the medicines. Monsters could be handled by Guild rules; disputes with humans had to follow Caskonia law — no private justice.

The client was a man named Noern. Nearly fifty, an apothecary, hair already greying, his face carrying the steady tiredness left by long nights over a brewing pot. He was not tall, but his fingers were perfectly steady; the way he checked the seals on his medicine boxes looked like a man stitching a wound.

"I asked for the two of you. Not so I could watch you bicker." Noern stood in the courtyard behind the tavern, looking at Ayla and Rayne planted on either side of him like two pieces of timber that thoroughly disliked one another. "Solaist hasn't many escorts free at the moment. Mister Rayne knows the East Road, and his name is enough to make some short-sighted bandits think twice. Miss Ayla is good at scouting and at handling trouble at range — and she just cleared the lumber yard, which says her form is good. My medicines can't be delayed. There are more than a dozen patients in Imeir waiting on them."

Rayne grunted. "I don't know about her form. Her tongue's sharp enough."

Ayla slung her bow over her shoulder. "I don't know about your form either. The tavern's oak door looks afraid of you."

Noern was silent for a moment, then looked at Bern.

Bern's face didn't move. "They're not always like this."

They were exactly like this.

The contract called for departure at dawn the next day. Ayla checked her quiver, bought two extra bundles of plain arrows and a healing potion. Rayne re-honed his axe and stuffed a few spare iron buckles into the strap of his shield. When the city gates opened at dawn, Solaist still lay under thin fog. The bakers' ovens had only just been lit along the streets, and the bells of distant villages mixed with the crowing of cocks. The cart waited beyond the east gate, the bed covered in waxed canvas, the axles freshly oiled. The horse was a chestnut veteran of decent stamina. His name was Doudou.

When Rayne heard the name, he stared at the horse for a long moment.

Ayla asked, "You have a problem with Doudou too?"

Rayne said, "I'm only thinking — if we get jumped, would yelling 'protect Doudou' hurt morale."

Ayla looked at the horse and answered seriously, "I'd say it would motivate people more than yelling 'protect you.'"

Noern bent his head over his medicine boxes, doing his best to pretend he hadn't heard.

When they left Solaist, the sun had only just cleared the city wall. The first stretch of the East Road was passable enough, with the wheat fields and beet plots common to Caskonia at the verges, the occasional villager passing with a sheep, or a child crouched at a ditch trying to catch a frog. Ayla walked on the right side of the cart, eyes sweeping the long grass and roadside woods. Rayne walked ahead and to the left, axe shouldered, his stride steady as a moving redoubt.

For the first two hours, they barely spoke.

In the third hour Rayne couldn't keep it in. "You keep watching the right. Think the monsters queue up to come from there?"

Ayla didn't turn her head. "You're on the left. If anything comes from the left, either you'll scare it off or you'll block my line of sight, and I won't see it."

Rayne grinned. "Is admitting I'm useful so hard?"

"Admitting you block my line of sight isn't."

Sitting on the cart-bench, Noern let out his first sigh of the day.

Around midday they hit their first trouble. A wolf pack, driven to the road by hunger — seven, dull-coated, ribs pushing through. They didn't attack at once. They paced the edge of the long grass, shadowing the cart like a low ring of shadows. Doudou snorted nervously; Noern tightened his grip on the reins.

Rayne lifted the axe off his shoulder. "I've got it."

"Don't." Ayla's hand stopped him. "They're only hungry, not rabid. Wolves don't usually attack humans on their own."

"You plan to reason with them?"

"I plan to make sure they know this meal isn't worth it."

Ayla drew an arrow with a small empty glass bulb tied behind the head — packed with strong horseradish powder and a small measure of spider-gland fluid. She nocked it, picked a stone in front of the pack, and shot. The bulb burst with a snap. A cloud of stinging scent spread on the wind. The wolves yelped, fell back. Rayne took the moment to swing the back of his axe into a roadside boulder. The crack of stone like a thunderclap finished what the powder had started.

When the wolves were gone, Rayne glanced at her.

"Potion arrow?"

"Cheap version." Ayla put the bow up. "Real potion arrows cost too much. This kind only stings the nose."

"It worked."

Ayla looked faintly surprised. "Were you just complimenting me?"

Rayne's face went immediately stone. "I was complimenting the horseradish."

The corner of her mouth moved, almost imperceptibly.

The exchange did not make them friends. But it let Noern breathe a little easier — at least these two, much as they sniped at each other, knew how to cooperate when something with teeth showed up.

Before evening they reached the first abandoned waystation. Half the roof had fallen in. Old torch brackets from a bygone legion still clung to the walls, and weeds had taken the courtyard. By the plan, this was where they would spend the night — to push on meant entering the Dark Birch Forest, and no one drove a cart through the Dark Birch Forest without moonlight.

Rayne saw to clearing the perimeter. Ayla strung fine bell-wires at the four corners of the waystation. Noern shifted the medicine boxes inside, where it was dry. The light slowly bled out of the sky; somewhere far off, the muffled groan of zombies. Rayne lit a fire. The light caught the side of his face — and the worn crest on his shield.

Ayla glanced once and didn't ask.

Rayne noticed her glance. His voice came hard. "Laugh if you want."

"Why would I laugh?"

"Plenty of people think wearing an old family crest is stupid. Especially after the family's gone."

Ayla was quiet for a moment, then pushed a stick further into the fire.

"That's not stupid," she said. "That's remembering."

Rayne went still.

The fire snapped and popped. It was the first time they had looked at each other without barbs in it.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 4

Chapter 4: Dark Birch Forest, Old Wounds

————————

In the deep of the night, the bells rang.

The sound was light — light enough that Ayla snapped awake without anything in between. There was no transition from sleep to waking; her hand was already on the bow, her body already low against the wall. Rayne was up too, the axe across his knees, like a bear that had opened its eyes by the fire.

There were no wolf-howls outside, no shuffling zombie steps. Only a fine, scrabbling movement — claws over wooden beams and stone, coming from the broken places in the roof.

Spiders.

Ayla raised a hand to keep Noern still and crept toward the window. In the moonlight, a long black leg dangled over the edge of the roof. Then a second. Then a third. At least four spiders moving along the eaves — one of them with a faint green pattern on its abdomen, almost certainly out of the abandoned mineshaft nearby, and far more venomous than the common kind.

Rayne whispered, "I'll take them."

Ayla's brow drew together. "Swing an axe in here and you'll bury the three of us with the spiders."

"Then you shoot?"

"Wait."

Instead of loosing at once, she pulled three short arrows out of the quiver, oil-soaked rags wound at the rear. Rayne caught on, picked up the torch, held it for her. Flame licked along the heads. Ayla shot the three in quick succession — into the grass at three different points outside. The fire-light pulled the spiders down from the roof at once, and that movement laid them right in the line that would open as Rayne went through the door.

The axe came across in a flat sweep. The first spider was driven into the wall. The second leapt at Rayne's flank — but Ayla's arrow was already through its head. The third, the venomous one, jumped fastest. It cleared the fire-line and went straight for the window where Noern stood. Ayla turned to nock again, but a chunk of rotten wood from the roof above struck her wrist; the arrow grazed the spider's belly and missed.

Rayne couldn't turn back in time.

The venomous spider crashed through the window-frame, fang aimed at Noern. Ayla bit her lip and threw herself in. Her left arm caught the strike. The instant the venom soaked into the cut in her bracer, the whole side of her arm went numb. Through the pain she pulled the boot-knife and drove it into the spider's eye. It rolled over, legs curling and spasming.

The fight had lasted seconds.

When it stopped, the air outside the waystation reeked of scorched grass; the room was a mess. Noern was at Ayla's arm at once, slicing the bracer open to look. The skin had already gone the colour of bruised iron. Her face was white, but she made no sound.

Rayne stood beside them, fists clenched.

"You could have stepped back," he said.

Ayla raised her eyes. "And let the apothecary be skewered? Mission failed?"

"That's not what I meant."

"Then what?"

Rayne opened his mouth. Nothing came out.

Noern took an antidote from the medicine box, then thinned a regeneration potion and poured it onto the wound a little at a time. Ayla drew breath sharply through her teeth. Rayne crouched and held out a clean strip of cloth, the gesture as awkward as a man tending a wound for the first time.

She took it. Her voice low: "Thanks."

Rayne grunted. "Don't read into it. I just don't want Bern saying a Gold can't keep an Iron alive."

"Don't worry." Ayla closed her eyes. "I'll tell him I just didn't dodge."

"Have you always been like this?" Rayne asked, suddenly.

"Like what?"

"Stepping in front of things."

Ayla didn't answer for a long time. The firelight on her bone-white face caught something behind her eyes that ran deeper than pain.

"When I was small I lived in a little village west of Caskonia," she said at last. "Not big. A few wheat fields, a very loud bell tower, and an old archer who always said he'd shot a phantom out of the air when he was young. He was my father."

Rayne didn't break in.

"That autumn, raiders came. Not an army, only a small band, with a witch and a few war-trained beasts that had been broken wrong. They wanted grain. They wanted medicine. They wanted people. The village hadn't enough of any of it. My father took a few of the watchmen and stood against them. I was young. I could only crouch on the granary roof and shake, and watch him stand at the door and put an arrow through the leader's shoulder."

She told it calmly. The calmer she was, the harder it was to breathe.

"Then they set the fires. The granary went up. The smoke was thick. My mother pushed me out of the back window and told me to run along the irrigation ditch. She said don't look back. I didn't look back. The next morning, I woke by the river. Seven people were left in the whole village."

Rayne's face was tight.

Ayla looked down at her bandaged arm. "After that I learned to shoot. Learned to see things before they reached me. Learned to put myself in the place that takes the hit. Not because I'm brave. Because I've already looked back too many times."

The waystation was very quiet.

Noern quietly returned the bottles to the box and didn't intrude. Rayne watched the fire; it lit and unlit whatever was in his eyes.

After a long pause he said, "Your father would be proud of your archery."

Ayla smiled — light, brief.

"You forgot to say horseradish this time."

Rayne let out a low laugh as well.

After that night, Ayla still found Rayne crude, stubborn, and full of himself. But she no longer thought he was only a big man with an axe.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 5

Chapter 5: Axe in the Rain

————————

The next morning the rain began.

Spring rain in Caskonia rarely warns. It started as a few cold drops on the cart's canvas, then thickened into a sheet that washed the grass on either side of the road into deep green. The East Road turned to mud; the wheels sank into soft soil more than once. Rayne had to set his shoulder to the cart, and Ayla led Doudou ahead, hunting for harder, stonier ground.

They argued less. They didn't quite stop.

"Could you not lead the horse so fast while I'm in the mud?" Rayne said, water running down his temple. "You're not in a fight."

Ayla looked back. "Could you push the cart with technique? Not every problem yields to shouting."

"Half of them do."

"The other half are the ones you started."

Noern, on the cart, hugging his medicine box, had given up sighing.

Before noon they came to a low valley. The road cut between two earthen slopes hung with rain-dark birches. The moment Ayla stepped into the mouth of the valley, she raised her hand for halt.

Rayne stopped at once.

This time he didn't argue.

Rain covered a great deal of sound, but Ayla still heard what wasn't rain — a light rattling of bone, very faint, from the top of the left slope. She looked up. Through the trees, white shapes moved.

Skeletons.

"Cover the cart," she said.

The first arrow came down from the slope and stuck in Rayne's shield. Then a second, then a third. Rayne raised the shield in front of the cart; metal rang against metal in the rain. Ayla rolled to a roadside stone and shot back. Rain dragged at her sight and her bowstring; her first arrow only shattered a skeleton's shoulder-bone.

"Three on the left ridge!" she shouted.

Rayne didn't answer. He charged the slope through the falling arrows, shield up, axe trailing behind, dragging a deep groove through the mud. The skeletons turned to him and drew together. Ayla used the moment — two quick arrows, two skeletons' leg-bones gone. Rayne hit the crest, axe in a wide swing, and broke the third one bow and ribs together.

But there was a fourth, behind the slope.

It wore broken leather and an enchanted bow that gave a faint glow. It didn't aim at Rayne. It aimed at the cart. The arrow came through the rain straight at the medicine box in Noern's arms.

Rayne leapt down from the ridge and put his body in front of the arrow.

The arrowhead drove into his right shoulder, the enchantment burning along the wound. He grunted but did not fall. Reverse-handed, he hurled the iron hammer-cap at the butt of his axe; it cracked the skeleton's skull.

The fight ended. The rain didn't.

They found a rock overhang to wait it out. Noern bit on a strap and pulled the arrow. Rayne's forehead cords stood out. The enchanted burn had blackened the flesh around the wound; the healing potion smoked thinly when it hit.

Ayla stood at the edge of the overhang, fingers still on her bow. She looked calm. Rayne saw her face was tight.

"Hey," he said.

She didn't turn.

"I'm not dead."

"I noticed."

"Then why the face?"

She turned at last, voice low. "You can raise the shield. You don't have to take it on the shoulder."

Rayne went still. Then he smiled.

"That sounds familiar."

Ayla glared.

Rayne stopped smiling. He looked down at the worn crest on his shield. "It was aimed at the box. Box breaks, the patients in Imeir don't get their medicine. Mission fails. Then you'd yell at me anyway."

"I wouldn't yell at you for not throwing your life away."

"It wasn't bravery," Rayne said.

This time Ayla said nothing.

Rain filled the world outside the overhang. Noern, finished with the wound, went to check the cart, knowing better than to stay. Rayne leaned against the rock, looking out at the grey-white wall of water as if at something far away.

"I wasn't a sellsword before," he said. "My father was a watch-officer of the northern border garrison of Caskonia. Not a noble, but the family had a shield, a crest, and a rule older than any of us: the man at the front does not step back first."

He raised his left arm a little. The old steel shield rested beside him.

"When I was sixteen, a village in the north was overrun. Not an ordinary night raid — a disaster brought on by inhabitants. Zombies, skeletons, spiders, and a raiding beast someone had loosed from somewhere. The garrison was late. My father took the men of the village who could lift a weapon and held the bridge so the women and children could cross."

His voice was lower than usual.

"I was on the bridge too. I had a hatchet. My hands shook. He pushed this shield into mine and told me to take a small girl across the river. I did. When I turned back, the bridge was gone. The water was fast. The fire was huge. All I could see of him was his axe stuck in the bank."

Ayla sat slowly down across from him.

"And the girl?"

Rayne shook his head. "Died on the road the next day. Wound too deep. Not enough medicine. That was the first time I understood that some people aren't killed by monsters. They're killed by medicine that comes too late, by reinforcements that come too late, by order that comes too late."

He looked toward the cart. "That's why I don't like escort jobs. And why I keep taking them."

Ayla said quietly, "Your father would be proud of you, too."

Rayne didn't answer at once. After a moment he turned his face away and muttered, "Don't borrow my line."

Ayla's mouth lifted. "I was complimenting your father."

By the time the rain stopped, evening had come. Rayne's shoulder still hurt. Ayla's poisoned arm wasn't quite healed. But when they took the road again, the rhythm between them was easier than the morning's — Ayla slowed when Rayne shifted the axe to his other shoulder, and Rayne stepped a little forward to break the wind whenever she held the bow drawn for too long.

They still mocked each other.

Only the words no longer carried only thorns.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 6

Chapter 6: Night in the Old Mine

————————

On the third night they slid half a li off the East Road and made camp in the mouth of an abandoned mine.

It wasn't the choice they would have wanted. The wind was too high to risk an open fire. The mine mouth still had its old timber bracing; inside, the tunnel was shallow. Ayla had checked the floor for tracks — nothing recent. Rayne stacked stones at the entrance, leaving a slit they could watch the outside through. Noern tied Doudou to the innermost beam and threw a blanket over him.

Dinner was hard bread, baked potatoes, and a little salted meat. Rayne ate quickly. Ayla ate slowly. Noern ate while reading down his medicine ledger, as if he expected a spider to come and steal a crate of nether wart while his eyes were closed.

"Why did you become a sellsword?" Rayne asked her, suddenly.

Ayla looked up. "For the money."

"Besides money."

"To stay alive."

"Besides staying alive."

She was silent for a moment. "So that one day I won't have to run."

Rayne didn't press.

Ayla wrapped the rest of her potato back in paper and sat with her arms around her knees by the fire. "After my village burned, I was taken in by a caravan. Not bad. Not good either. I watched their goods, I stood watch, I drove off wolves who tried for the sheep, in exchange for food and the corner of a wagon I could sleep in. Later, on the edge of a sand-flat, raiders found us. I told the leader to take a different route. He didn't believe me. That night they hid the most expensive cargo under the wagons and pushed me out the back to draw the chasers."

Rayne's head came up sharply.

Ayla spoke as if of someone else. "I didn't die. I'm good at running, and at hiding. The next morning I stole an old bow and half a quiver of arrows and walked to Solaist on my own. The Mercenary Guild at least writes its rules on the wall — what you'll be paid, what you'll do, what you won't do. It's not kind. But it's clear. Clear is more reliable than kind."

Rayne said, low, "What was the caravan called?"

"Why?"

"In case I run into them on the road, so I can pay my respects."

Ayla looked at him, then suddenly laughed. It was the first real laugh Rayne had seen from her — not mocking, not cold, but brief and bright. The fire of the mine made the always-tight line of her face soft.

"A Gold-Rank planning to break Guild charter for something ten years gone?"

"I can pay my respects within the law."

"How?"

"Buy all their goods. Then not pay."

"That's robbery."

"So I'm still working out the legal version."

Beside them Noern coughed, like a man reminding two people not to treat their apothecary as air. There was a smile in his eyes too.

Late in the night, something sounded from the deep of the mine.

It wasn't wind. It wasn't aging timber. It was a deep, wet breathing, mixed with the scrape of claws on stone. Doudou pricked uneasy and laid his ears flat. Ayla took up the bow. Rayne stood.

A cave bear came out of the dark.

Drawn, probably, by the firelight and the smell of food. Larger than a common brown bear, its shoulders covered in dark grey moss and mine-dust, its eyes a clouded yellow in the firelight. It wasn't undead. It wasn't a magic-thing. Just a hungry, hurt animal whose den had been pushed into a shrinking corner by men's old workings.

Ayla's first arrow struck the stone in front of it — a warning. The bear paused for one beat, then came on with a low growl.

Rayne met it. Shield against bear-paw, his whole body slid back half a step. Ayla's second arrow took it in the shoulder but didn't go through the thick fur. Noern hauled Doudou back toward the entrance — and was blocked by fallen timber.

The tunnel was too narrow for the axe to be fully swung. The bear used its weight, pressing on. Rayne's shoulder wound split during the struggle; blood soaked through the bandage. Ayla saw it. She switched to armour-piercing arrows and waited for the moment the bear opened its jaws to roar. The arrow brushed past Rayne's ear and stuck above the soft palate of the bear's mouth.

The bear reared back in pain.

Rayne took the moment, low growl and shield-cant to throw the paw aside, and brought the axe down short into the bear's foreleg. It roared loud enough to bring stone down from the roof. The fire flared and dimmed.

"Step back!" Ayla shouted.

He didn't. He knew that the moment he gave ground, the bear would go for Noern and the medicines. He braced himself across the tunnel mouth, feet half-buried in mud. Ayla saw the blood at his shoulder running faster, and her chest went tight. She drew out the last of her expensive potion arrows — the instant-damage one she had been saving for the worst.

She held her breath.

Firelight, rain, the bear's roar, Rayne's panting — all of it went distant. The world narrowed to a single line.

The arrow flew. It threaded the impossibly thin gap between Rayne and the bear and drove into the bear's neck. The potion broke open. Dark-purple lines spread along the veins. The bear's strength dropped quickly. Rayne forced the axe in once more, and the bear retreated, dragging its hurt leg, into the dark of the mine. It did not come back.

Rayne sat down against the wall, breathing hard.

Ayla rushed to him, knelt, tore the bandage open. "Are you mad? I told you to step back!"

Rayne grinned, though his face had gone white. "You said I block your line of sight. Did I block it cleanly that time?"

"Shut up."

Her hand was shaking — not from the bear, but from the moment when she had really thought he was about to die.

Noern handed her a healing potion. She took it, working fast, but very carefully tipped it into the wound. The pink shimmer sank into the torn flesh; Rayne hissed but did not joke again.

"Ayla," he said quietly.

"Don't talk."

"That last shot was beautiful."

Her eyes pricked, but her voice stayed hard. "Don't compliment the horseradish."

Rayne laughed under his breath. He raised his unwounded left hand, hesitated, and very lightly touched the back of her hand. She didn't move it away.

Outside the mine, after the rain, a few stars came through the cloud. The far moon hung in a gap, pale and quiet — and if one looked closely, the rim of the disc carried a thin, almost invisible cast of purple, like a prophecy that had not yet been spoken aloud. That night they took watches in turn, but neither slept much. When the fire was almost out, Rayne dropped his cloak across Ayla. Ayla didn't say thank you. She only pulled half of it back over his shoulders.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 7

Chapter 7: Dawn Before Imeir

————————

When they were a dozen kilometres from Imeir Town, the East Road opened out.

Orchards and small pasture-strips appeared on either side of the road; in the distance, the windmills of Imeir came into view. The roofs there were mostly dark planking; at the town entrance stood a small bell tower whose chime was particularly gentle in the morning mist. Noern visibly relaxed. Even Doudou seemed to know the end was near; his step quickened.

But the closer the goal, the easier it is to make a mistake.

The attack came in the last patch of low woodland.

Not a swarm of monsters — a single wounded raiding beast. It must have been hit by a hunter's crossbow once; its left eye was blind, and half a broken spear still stuck out of its back. Long pain had made it manic; the moment it saw the cart, it broke from the brush. Half-wolf, half-boar, with curved tusks and stiff bristles down the shoulders, it moved faster than any wounded thing should.

Doudou shrieked. The cart almost went over.

Rayne went forward first, shield up. The beast smashed into the shield; the impact pushed Rayne back several paces, his heels carving deep furrows in the mud. Ayla leapt onto a tree-root at the edge of the road and put three arrows into its legs in quick succession. Two struck. They only made it angrier.

"It fears fire!" Noern shouted. "Look at the bristles on its back!"

Ayla snatched a fire arrow at once, but the wood was damp from the rain, and the head was slow to take. The beast used the second to run round Rayne and charge the cart. Noern threw himself in front of the medicine box. Doudou tried desperately to back up, but the cartwheels jammed in a rut.

Rayne could not catch it.

Ayla didn't hesitate. She jumped down from the root and put herself between cart and beast, loosing the only-half-burning fire arrow at point-blank range. The head went into the beast's shoulder; only a small cluster of sparks flared. The beast didn't stop. A tusk caught Ayla across the side, and she went flying.

She hit the ground. Her sight went black.

"Ayla!"

Rayne's roar broke open inside her chest. Through the dark she saw him drop the shield, take the axe in both hands, and hurl himself at the beast. There was nothing pretty in that strike — only pure rage and pure fear. The axe came down across the side of the beast's neck and pinned it into the mud. It tried to claw back to its feet. Ayla forced herself up on one elbow, gritted her teeth, and put her last armour-piercing arrow into it just below the blind eye.

It stopped moving.

Rayne almost fell to her side. His hand reached for her, then froze halfway, as if he were afraid he was too heavy, too clumsy, that he would break something.

"Where are you hurt?" His voice was more frightened than it had been in any fight.

Ayla could barely breathe. She still pushed out a sentence. "You... you look like you're being chased by a Creeper."

"Don't joke."

"That line's... usually mine."

Noern came running and went to work at her side. The tusk hadn't punched through any organ, but a long tear ran along her flank, and she had lost a fair amount of blood. When the healing potion went on, Ayla bit down on Rayne's wrist. She meant to let go at once, then realised Rayne had reverse-clasped her hand.

His hand was big and hot, the palm full of callus.

"Don't sleep," he said.

"I'm not."

"Look at me."

"You're too ugly."

"Then curse me."

Ayla wanted to laugh. The wound pulled it into a small hiss. She looked at him and was struck for the first time by how completely he was not what she had thought. The crudeness was armour. The mocking mouth was a shield. All the shouting was about keeping things outside. But when danger really came, he was always the one in front, even with his own body in tatters.

She said quietly, "Rayne."

"Mm?"

"That family rule of your father's — don't always carry it alone."

Her voice was low and very clear. "The man at the front can also have someone standing beside him."

Wind moved through the low woodland; rainwater on the leaves dropped, slow. Rayne looked at her, and his throat moved, as if too much was caught in his chest. In the end he said, "Then the same goes for you. Don't always make yourself the last arrow in the line."

Ayla closed her eyes.

"Deal."

Noern finished bandaging, looked up, and saw their hands still joined. He silently bent over his bottles again, pretending to be busy.

Half an hour later they took the road again. Ayla rode on the cart, wearing Rayne's cloak. Rayne walked at the cart's side. Now and then he'd look at her, then quickly look away. Ayla noticed but didn't call him on it — until the third time, when she said, "The road's in front of you. Not on my face."

Rayne coughed. "I'm checking you haven't passed out."

"Done checking?"

"Still able to be annoyed. So, fine."

She laughed.

This time, Rayne laughed too.

## Solaist East Road: Chapter 8

Chapter 8: The Bells of Imeir Town

————————

Imeir Town received them just before noon.

The bell at the town entrance rang to tell the clinic the medicine had come. Several villagers came up to help unload. The town's apothecary ran out of the doorway and held the boxes of nether wart and glistering melon slices like life-rafts. In the clinic there was a child with a deep cough, a miner with a leg wrapped in bloodied bandages, and an old man with the dark socketed eyes of long poisoning. Noern did not rest. The moment he had stamped the contract, he rolled up his sleeves and stepped into the dispensary.

Ayla and Rayne stood at the clinic door for a moment, neither of them speaking.

The mission was over.

From the moment the parchment took the seal of the Imeir clinic, they were free to return to Solaist for payment. By Guild rules, this had been a successful joint Iron-and-Gold escort job. The recorder would write: route Solaist–Imeir, seventy kilometres, encounters with wolves, spiders, skeletons, a cave bear, and an unidentified raiding beast. Client and goods arrived safely. Performance — Excellent.

But for Ayla and Rayne, parchment had no room for what had really happened.

It had no room for a question of concern that had not been asked aloud in the rain. No room for a cloak shared at half-and-half in a mine. No room for a crude axeman admitting for the first time he was afraid to lose someone — or for a quiet archer permitting someone, for the first time, to take her hand.

Imeir put them up in a small inn next to the clinic. Out of the window you could see the windmills and a vegetable plot. Rayne's wound was the worse, and Noern more or less pinned him to a chair to redress it. Ayla sat on the windowsill, her bow across her lap, watching a few children chase chickens.

Rayne said, suddenly, "What rank do you want to reach, eventually?"

"Gold," Ayla said. "At least then you can pick the work and don't take everything that walks in."

"Diamond?"

"Too much trouble. I hear Diamonds get pulled into things the nobility wants done out of sight."

"Netherite?"

"Worse trouble. The name itself isn't something humans should be touching."

Rayne nodded. "Reasonable."

Ayla turned her head. "And you? Gold's already not bad."

"I used to want to climb," Rayne said, looking at his bandaged shoulder. "Thought a higher rank would prove I hadn't let down that shield. Then I noticed: rank goes up, the road gets longer. Each time you come back, the tavern's the same tavern, the chair's the same chair, no one waiting and no one asking."

Ayla looked at him.

Rayne fidgeted. "I'm not complaining."

"I know."

"And I'm not asking you to feel sorry for me."

"I don't."

"Then why are you looking at me?"

Ayla thought about it. "You're not bad to look at when you're not saying something stupid."

Rayne stared blankly. The tips of his ears went slowly red.

Ayla had just discovered that needling Rayne didn't always need sarcasm. Sometimes plain truth worked better.

In the evening, Noern finally came out of the dispensary. He could barely stand, but he insisted on inviting them to dinner at the town tavern. Imeir's tavern was much smaller than Solaist's; dried sweet berries hung along the walls, and a few redstone lamps gave a low light. The townsfolk, hearing two sellswords had escorted the medicine in, came up to clink glasses. Someone recognised Rayne as the Solaist Gold and at once asked for a story; a few children clung to Ayla and asked if it was really true she could shoot a skeleton through the eye in the rain.

She answered, "No."

The children sighed in disappointment.

Ayla added, "In rain you aim for the joints. Eye's too small. Wastes arrows."

Their faces went up several notches in admiration.

Rayne, watching from a little way off, couldn't help laughing. Ayla noticed, raised an eyebrow. Rayne lifted his cup, conceding.

Late in the night the town bell rang nine times. Noern went back to the clinic to keep working; the townsfolk drifted home. Ayla and Rayne walked out of the tavern side by side. Outside, the moon was bright on Imeir's quiet flagstones. Somewhere a villager was shutting the gate of the sheep-pen, the windmill turned slowly, and the air smelled of herbs, soil, and baking bread.

They stopped under the old oak at the edge of town.

The road back to Solaist could wait until morning. Tonight, neither of them had a job, no monsters, no wound that demanded immediate attention. Only the moon, and two breaths that suddenly went very quiet.

Rayne spoke first. "Ayla."

"Mm."

"When we get back, if Bern puts us on another job together —"

"You want to file a complaint?"

"No."

He drew a deep breath, like a man bracing for a worse beast than the cave bear. "I mean, if you're willing — we could be fixed partners."

Ayla looked at him.

Rayne added quickly, "Just on jobs. You shoot well. I cover well. Better efficiency, easier pay split. Don't read into it."

Ayla, slow: "What is there to read into?"

Rayne stuck.

Moonlight caught his face, and there was no corner left for the noisy, crude Gold-Rank sellsword who always raised an axe before he raised a sentence. He had cornered himself with his own words, and at last he gave up the bad excuses entirely.

"I don't want to see you only on jobs," he said.

Ayla's expression didn't change. But her hand on the bow tightened, just a little.

Rayne went on. "I know I've a foul tongue. I've a foul temper. The first time we met, we nearly fought."

"Not nearly," Ayla said. "If Bern had been one beat slower, that table would have been kindling."

"Right." Rayne smiled crookedly. "But on this road, I found you irritating."

Ayla's eyes narrowed.

"Irritating, in that I kept asking why the skeletons shot at you, why the spiders bit you, why the bear came at you and not me. Irritating, in that the moment you got hurt I wanted to chop apart everything within reach. Irritating, in that I'm standing here now, knowing I'm bad with words, and I want to say it anyway."

His voice dropped.

"I love you, Ayla."

The wind paused.

Ayla looked at him for a long time and didn't answer. She thought of the arrogant Gold-Rank in the Solaist tavern, of the shoulder that had taken arrows in the rain, of the hand in the mine that hadn't known where to settle, and of her own line: the man at the front can have someone beside him.

There had been a long time when she did not believe in the word "beside." The people beside her had died, or betrayed her, or pushed her out of a smoke-filled window, or thrown her to chasers in a caravan night-raid. She had learned to walk a road alone, to stand watch alone, to bind her wounds alone, and to tell herself not to look back.

But Rayne stood there, awkward and tense and bandaged, like a wall of stone that did not know how to be gentle.

Ayla suddenly felt that not every backward look would show ruin.

Sometimes, looking back, you see someone who is still standing there.

"Rayne," she said.

"Mm."

"After this, you say less about me hiding behind people."

"I swear."

"Less taking out your temper on doors."

"I'll try."

"You take orders from me on jobs."

"That one's situational."

Ayla turned to walk away.

Rayne panicked. "Wait — fine, I agree! I'll take them! Most of the time I'll take them!"

She stopped. And she laughed, finally — a small laugh that was no louder than a torch lighting an old mine. Rayne stared at her until she walked back, took him by the collar, and pulled him down.

She kissed him.

It wasn't the long, ornate kiss of a bard's song. No roses. No applause. No fated swell of music. Only the old oak at the edge of Imeir Town, the lamps still burning over at the clinic, an old horse named Doudou snorting once in the stable, and two battered sellswords admitting, after one ordinary escort, that they were no longer ordinary to one another.

Rayne went stiff like a man hit with a binding spell.

Ayla let him go and said, quiet, "Fixed partners — yes."

Rayne hadn't recovered.

She added, "Lovers — also yes."

His face went blank for an instant; then, like a man finally hearing his own language, he lit up entirely. He wanted to hold her tight and stopped because of her side. He wanted to laugh out loud and stopped because half the town would wake. In the end he carefully took her hand, like something rarer than a Diamond-Rank badge.

"So when we get back to Solaist —"

"First we collect the pay," Ayla said.

Rayne smiled. "And then?"

"You buy me a drink."

"Of course."

"Then we go and find Bern."

"Why?"

Ayla looked west. The Solaist East Road stretched on under the moon, its end hidden in the dark. She knew the road wouldn't grow safer because of one feeling, and that there would be more monsters, more wounds, more nights that would never be written into a job report. But she no longer felt she had to walk it alone.

"Tell him," she said, "that any job that needs a Gold who blocks the line of sight and an Iron who knows her horseradish — should be sent to us first."

Rayne laughed under his breath.

"He'll raise the rate."

"Let him."

They stood together under Imeir's moon, fingers slowly tightening. Far off, the bell tower struck once more, like a seal pressed onto a story not yet entered into the Guild's records.

They didn't yet know how the adventures to come would change their lives. They knew only that the Solaist East Road was long, and that the way back might still hold wolves, skeletons, rain and mud.

This time, Ayla was no longer just an Iron-Rank archer.

Rayne was no longer just a Gold-Rank axeman.

They were the people standing beside one another.

## Solaist East Road: Epilogue

Epilogue: New Rumors in the Tavern

————————

A few days later, a new rumour took hold in the Solaist Mercenary Guild tavern.

Some said Rayne hadn't kicked the door open on his return — he'd actually pushed it. Some said when Ayla picked her next job at the board, Rayne had stood beside her and shoved a few drunks out of the way. Some said Bern, after reading the Imeir verdict, had been silent for a long while, then crossed out "Temporary Pairing" and written in "Recommended Long-Term Partners."

Charcoal-Rank novices argued about it endlessly. Some said the Iron archer had finally tamed the Gold axeman; others said Ayla must have slipped him a Slowness potion on the road, since no one else could make a man that big speak in a low voice.

Bern, polishing a glass, only smirked when he heard.

"Don't talk nonsense," he said. "It isn't a question of who tamed whom."

A novice asked, "Then what is it?"

Bern looked up. In the corner of the tavern, Rayne was nudging a cup of ale toward Ayla, and Ayla, without looking, was tightening the loose end of his bandage. They were still bickering, still finding fault with the way the other fought. But they sat very close — too close to fit a third chair between them.

The old barkeep set the glass back on its rack, a faint amusement in his voice.

"It's two people," he said, "who would have walked forward alone. And finally learned to walk side by side."

Outside, night drew slowly down over Solaist. The far moon was quiet and pale, not yet stained with the purple cast of the Cursed Moon. The fire in the tavern was high; the parchments on the job board stirred in the warm air. In one corner, on a yellowed scroll, a piece of charcoal had roughly sketched a single solitary distant tower, and a dark forest stamped over and over with the mark of 'Forbidden.'

The story had not yet reached that place.
